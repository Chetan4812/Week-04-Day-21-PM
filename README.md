### 3. Explain

**What is Bias?**

Bias is the error introduced by making overly simplistic assumptions in the learning algorithm. A high-bias model cannot capture the true patterns in the data, resulting in underfitting — it performs poorly on both training and test data.

*Example: Fitting a straight line (degree 1 polynomial) to data generated by sin(2πx).*

**What is Variance?**

Variance is the error from the model being overly sensitive to small fluctuations in the training data. A high-variance model memorises the training noise rather than learning the underlying signal, resulting in overfitting — very low training error but high test error.

*Example: Fitting a degree-10 polynomial to only 40 samples.*

**Where does the optimal model lie?**

The optimal model sits at the sweet spot where the sum of Bias² + Variance is minimised:

> **Total Error = Bias² + Variance + Irreducible Noise**

*   As model complexity increases, Bias² decreases but Variance increases.
*   The optimal complexity is where test error stops decreasing and starts to rise.
*   In our experiment, polynomial **degree 2–3** gives the best generalisation.

| Model | Bias | Variance | Status |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Degree 1 | High | Low | ❌ Underfitting |
| Degree 2–3 | Medium | Medium | ✅ Optimal |
| Degree 10 | Low | High | ❌ Overfitting |

**Q3 — Explain bias–variance tradeoff. What happens in underfitting and overfitting?**

### Underfitting (High Bias):
*   Model is too simple to capture the underlying pattern.
*   Training error is high; test error is also high.
*   Fix: increase model complexity, add more features, reduce regularisation.

### Overfitting (High Variance):
*   Model memorises training noise instead of learning the signal.
*   Training error is very low; test error is high (large gap).
*   Fix: regularisation (L1/L2), more training data, reduce model complexity, dropout.
